AI is becoming indispensable.

The more we use it, the more uses we find for it. Just as a lever and fulcrum allow us to move more weight than we ever could alone, AI amplifies the amount of work we can accomplish.

That's a good thing.

The problem is that many of us have accidentally outsourced our thinking about AI architecture to the latest frontier models.

For the last two years, we've gotten comfortable reaching for the biggest model available. Frontier reasoning models have become the chrome-plated hammer in our toolbox, and every problem suddenly looks like a nail.

Need a summary? Use GPT-5.

Need a workflow? Use GPT-5.

Need an agent? Use GPT-5.

Need a customer service bot? Use GPT-5.

Need to classify documents? Use GPT-5.

Then the bill arrives.

And suddenly we remember something architects have known for decades:

Architecture still matters.

## We've Seen This Before

For years, architects preached a simple lesson:

Don't use a distributed system when a monolith will do.

Yet there was a time when distributed systems became the shiny new hammer. Organizations reached for microservices whether they needed them or not. Every problem, large or small, got the same solution.

AI is creating a similar moment.

Don't use a frontier model when a smaller model will do.

The problem isn't the price per token.

The problem is treating every AI task as if it requires the most expensive intelligence available.

## The Agentic Opportunity

One of the most important lessons emerging from agentic systems is that not every step requires a genius.

Consider a typical customer support workflow:

- Route the request
- Extract intent
- Search the knowledge base
- Draft a response
- Escalate when necessary

Most of those tasks don't require a frontier reasoning model.

Smaller, faster, less expensive models can often handle:

- Classification
- Routing
- Extraction
- Formatting
- Validation

Reserve frontier models for what they do best:

- Complex reasoning
- Ambiguous decisions
- Strategic recommendations
- Novel problem solving

The result?

The same customer outcome.

At a fraction of the cost.

## The Bigger Lesson

We've lived through this before.

When organizations first moved to the cloud, many workloads landed on oversized infrastructure. Nobody wanted to be the person who underpowered a system that might suddenly go viral.

So we provisioned big.

Then the bill arrived.

Over time, we learned to optimize.

We learned about:

- Storage tiers
- Compute sizing
- Autoscaling
- Caching
- CDN strategies

Nobody deploys a 64-core server to host a static website.

Yet many AI architectures effectively do exactly that.

Every request gets routed to the most expensive model available.

We're overprovisioning intelligence.

## A Practical Framework

When evaluating an AI workload, ask a simple question:

Does this task require recognition or reasoning?

Recognition tasks include:

- Classification
- Extraction
- Tagging
- Routing
- Search

Reasoning tasks include:

- Planning
- Synthesis
- Tradeoff analysis
- Strategy
- Complex troubleshooting

Recognition is rapidly becoming a commodity capability.

Reasoning remains premium.

Architect accordingly.

## The Future

The future isn't one frontier model running everything.

It's specialized models, tools, workflows, and agents working together.

The organizations that learn this first won't just reduce AI costs.

They'll build AI systems that scale.

We've spent the last twenty years learning not to overprovision infrastructure.

Why are we overprovisioning intelligence?
